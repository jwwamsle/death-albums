Ranking Death’s Main LPs (Worst to Best)

Death was an American death metal band formed by Chuck Schuldiner in the mid-1980s, widely regarded as pioneers of the genre. Their music evolved significantly over the course of their career, starting as raw, brutal death metal and gradually incorporating progressive elements, technical complexity, and philosophical lyrical themes. Their legacy endures as one of the cornerstones of extreme metal, influencing countless bands and shaping the sound of the genre for decades to come.

Who Were Death?

Emerging from Florida’s early death metal scene, Death brought an unparalleled intensity to the underground. Led by vocalist/guitarist Chuck Schuldiner, the band continually pushed the boundaries of heavy music. Their earliest records defined the raw brutality of the genre, while later albums introduced intricate song structures, thoughtful lyrics, and virtuoso musicianship. Death’s evolution is a testament to artistic growth—each album capturing a moment of expansion and reflection in Schuldiner’s musical vision.

7. Spiritual Healing (1990)

Theme & Color: Pale blues and clinical hues to reflect moral decay and exploitation in a supposedly healing environment. The artwork underscores twisted spirituality and corrupted morality.

Why #7?: Spiritual Healing represents a transitional phase. Musically, it’s more sophisticated than their debut, but it hasn’t fully embraced the advanced technicality and depth they would later achieve. Lyrically, the band began tackling social issues, stepping away from pure gore and horror. While it’s a crucial stepping stone, it’s overshadowed by the greater achievements to come.

The songwriting shows hints of the progressive streak that would define later works, yet the production still feels somewhat restrained. Nonetheless, it’s an important milestone marking where Death’s identity began to shift toward more thoughtful compositions.

6. Scream Bloody Gore (1987)

Theme & Color: Deep reds and black shadows evoke blood-splattered horror, reflecting the band’s earliest, most primitive form.

Why #6?: As their debut, Scream Bloody Gore is one of the first true death metal albums. Savage and unpolished, it captures a raw intensity unmatched at the time. Historically crucial, it’s a blueprint for the genre rather than the fully realized vision Death would later present.

This album’s ferocious simplicity defined early death metal’s sound, even if it would later be surpassed in complexity and depth by their subsequent releases.

5. Leprosy (1988)

Theme & Color: Flesh-toned pinks and sickly hues mirror the gruesome cover and unyielding exploration of disease and decay.

Why #5?: Leprosy refines the rawness of the debut, introducing sharper guitar work, more dynamic drumming, and better production. It’s a defining death metal record that begins to hint at the sophistication Death would eventually embrace.

The album marks a significant leap forward, yet it remains firmly rooted in the brute force of early death metal, not fully reaching the profound artistry of later works.

4. Individual Thought Patterns (1993)

Theme & Color: Subtle beiges and earth tones reflect introspection, human complexity, and philosophical depth, moving away from stark brutality into more nuanced intellectual territory.

Why #4?: Individual Thought Patterns is a technical masterstroke. Steve Di Giorgio’s fretless bass, intricate guitar lines, and philosophical lyrics merge to create a cerebral listening experience. It lays the groundwork for even more progressive directions while still maintaining a death metal core.

This album challenges the listener intellectually and musically, setting Death apart from their peers and moving them closer to their ultimate artistic heights.

3. Human (1991)

Theme & Color: Cool teals and blues signify introspection and the quest to understand human nature, bridging ferocity and sophistication.

Why #3?: Human is a watershed album, integrating complex time signatures and virtuosic musicianship. The addition of Cynic members Sean Reinert and Paul Masvidal brought a new level of technicality and creativity, blending brutality with innovation.

The result is a balanced masterpiece that marks Death’s transformation into a thinking person’s death metal band, paving the way for their most ambitious works.

2. The Sound of Perseverance (1998)

Theme & Color: Fiery reds and ambers reflect a molten core of ambition, as if forging the band’s final opus from molten metal.

Why #2?: The Sound of Perseverance is Death’s last album, synthesizing technicality, melody, and progressive ambition. Schuldiner’s vocals hit new highs, the compositions are intricate, and the overall feel is that of a band reaching its zenith of expression.

A near-perfect album that stands as a pinnacle of progressive death metal—just shy of the intangible brilliance found in their crowning achievement.

1. Symbolic (1995)

Theme & Color: Soft golds and warm neutrals convey timelessness and artistic maturity, transcending mere trends.

Why #1?: Symbolic is Death’s crowning achievement, balancing progressiveness, heaviness, and philosophical depth. Each track is meticulously crafted, blending intricate musicianship with emotional resonance.

It stands as a landmark album in metal history—intelligent, emotive, and endlessly influential. Symbolic represents what death metal can aspire to be: a profound musical journey beyond mere brutality.
